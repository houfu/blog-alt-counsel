# When “No Lawyers” Meets a 1,000-Paragraph Employment Judgment

`[2026] SGECT 1` made me pause because the Employment Claims Tribunals are supposed to be accessible, private, and lawyer-light. But this judgment runs long, canvasses difficult arguments on wrongful dismissal, disciplinary processes, discretionary bonuses, burden of proof, and the relationship between statutory dismissal claims and common law wrongful dismissal. The tribunal itself notes the institutional constraint: parties act in person, so tribunals do not get opposing legal submissions from counsel. That is the design. But is it still realistic? Large employers may have HR teams, in-house employment counsel, or outside lawyers shaping the case from behind the curtain. Employees usually do not. This post uses `[2026] SGECT 1` as the trigger to ask whether “no lawyers” really means equal access, or just invisible asymmetry. Then it demonstrates how Zeeker’s MCP can help a claimant, union officer, or small-team counsel level up: finding related authorities, mapping recurring arguments, and turning a dense employment judgment into a research path without pretending the tool replaces legal advice.

## Research Direction

Core question: when a tribunal system is designed for parties to appear in person, what happens when the law and factual record become too complex for that design?

The post should not become a general employment law case note. It should use the case to show the access-to-justice asymmetry:

- employers may have in-house HR, employment counsel, or outside counsel shaping the file before the tribunal;
- employees generally arrive with less legal scaffolding, even if union-assisted;
- published ECT reasoning now gives future claimants a map, but only if they can find and use it;
- Zeeker MCP can demonstrate the practical “level up” by surfacing related authorities and recurring arguments.
