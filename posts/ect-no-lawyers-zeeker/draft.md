I saw it first on LinkedIn, huddled between conversations between leading employment lawyers in Singapore. That's when you know it's big. 

Six employees won compensation after medical-benefit claims involving vitamins, supplements, and skincare. One employee had submitted 62 reimbursement claims worth nearly $10,000. Around 40 employees were dismissed after an internal review. The Employment Claims Tribunal (ECT) found that the employer had not proven dishonesty, recklessness, or wilful blindness. Justice served? I won't blame you for hesitating to answer. 

From a legal point of view, the hard question was not whether the employer had a good reason to terminate the employment. It was whether the employer proved the reason it chose to rely on: serious breaches committed intentionally, wilfully, or dishonestly. That is a different question. A more technical question. A question that depends on legal labels, evidential burdens, statutory wording, and remedies.

That is what made JGP v JGQ [2026] SGECT 1 interesting to me.

The Employment Claims Tribunals are meant to be accessible. Parties present their own cases without lawyers. Presumably without any lawyers, the tribunal can also take a more active approach, whether it is to encourage parties to settle, or to get to the heart of the issues sooner. Proceedings are private. The forum is supposed to be speedy, low-cost, and simpler than ordinary litigation.

The same problem cuts the other way too. If you are an employee hoping to prove wrongful dismissal, a good story is not enough. "I was treated unfairly" may be true. It may even be compelling. But the forum still has to ask what kind of wrongful dismissal claim this is, what has to be proven, what evidence matters, and what remedy is available.

Once you see that, you need research.

Not necessarily a 20-page memo. Not necessarily a full paid database dive at the first step. But some way to move from "the facts feel strong" to "these are the legal questions I actually need to understand".

The tribunal itself says the difficulty out loud. These cases had to be decided in a setting where parties act in person, so the tribunal does not get opposing counsel's arguments on legal questions. Yet the judgment is a long, careful decision about wrongful dismissal, disciplinary procedure, discretionary bonuses, burden of proof, the difference between common law wrongful dismissal and statutory wrongful dismissal, and what happens when the same phrase covers more than one legal idea.

That is the part I could not shake.



But "no lawyers in the room" does not mean "no legal advantage".

## What Happened

The facts are uncomfortable, which is why the public reaction is understandable.

In late 2023, the employer started an internal review of medical-benefit claims linked to a clinic anonymised as Clinic X. Employees had been claiming items like vitamins, supplements, and skincare products. The review led to a disciplinary process. Around 40 employees were eventually dismissed.

Eight filed claims in the ECT. One withdrew. Another proceeded before a different tribunal. Six related cases came before Tribunal Magistrate Jared Kang Chern Wey, who issued detailed written grounds using one claimant's case, Ms C's, as the main vehicle.

The numbers are not small:

- 62 reimbursement claims by Ms C over seven months
- almost $10,000 claimed for items including vitamins, supplements, and skincare
- about 40 employees dismissed after the wider review
- six successful related ECT claims before the same tribunal
- S$17,332 awarded to Ms C, plus costs and disbursements

The employer's case was not merely that the claims were wrong. It had framed the dismissals around serious breaches committed intentionally, wilfully, or dishonestly. The tribunal found that the employer had not proven dishonesty, recklessness, or wilful blindness. Ms C had committed a lesser wrong, but that did not amount to just cause or excuse for dismissal.

That distinction matters.

The point is not whether the employees were blameless. The point is that the difference between "wrong", "dismissible", and "proven dishonest" is legal work.

## The Public Read and the Lawyer Read

The public read is easy to understand.

You see "skincare", "$10,000", "medical benefits", and "compensation". Your instinct may be: how did the employees win? That was the shape of some business-facing commentary. Chris J Reed called the result a travesty and focused on the intuitive unfairness of claiming that much for vitamins and skincare.

The mainstream news version was narrower. CNA explained that the tribunal found the employer had not proven dishonesty. The Independent Singapore turned the lesson toward workplace practices and unclear policies.

Employment lawyers read the case differently.

Nicholas Ngo focused on the doctrinal problem: "wrongful dismissal" now lives in two places. In court, the common law claim is anchored in breach. In the ECT, the statutory test asks whether the dismissal was without just cause or excuse. That creates questions about no-reasons dismissals, evidential burden, and what an employer's silence now costs.

Clarence Ding took a different view. He thought the tribunal got it wrong and was glad the employer had filed applications to appeal. But even his disagreement helps the point. He still accepted that statutory wrongful dismissal claims have become complex and unclear, and called for more published judgments.

So the case has at least three readings:

- the moral frame: they claimed skincare
- the HR frame: policy controls and workplace practice failed
- the legal frame: what reason was given, what had to be proven, and what kind of wrongful dismissal claim is this?

This is where the ECT design starts to feel strained.

Not because the tribunal should suddenly become ordinary litigation. That would defeat the point. But because the same facts can produce radically different readings depending on whether the reader understands the legal architecture underneath.

And if understanding the legal architecture is necessary, then research becomes part of access.

## The Design Problem

When the ECT launched in 2017, the State Courts described it as a speedy, low-cost forum with simplified, judge-led procedures. No lawyers would be involved. The reason was practical: because procedures were simplified, the ECT would not be a forum for complicated employment claims.

That design is important.

If every employment claim required lawyers, many employees would never bring claims at all. Salary disputes and wrongful dismissal claims often sit in the painful middle: too important to ignore, too small to justify full litigation, too urgent to wait years.

Keeping the ECT lawyer-light is not wrong.

But [2026] SGECT 1 shows the irony. The tribunal published a 414-fragment judgment, with an annex analysing the law on dismissals in Singapore. It explained that the label "wrongful dismissal" now covers both a common law action and a statutory claim. It said the boundaries between substance, process, forum, and remedy are not consistently understood.

This is not the kind of complexity most people associate with a simplified tribunal.

The access gap is not only about who appears at the hearing. It starts earlier.

A large employer may have HR teams, compliance officers, internal escalation processes, in-house counsel, or outside employment lawyers shaping the file before anyone steps into the tribunal. Even if no lawyer appears, the investigation, disciplinary framing, correspondence, appeal handling, and evidence bundle may already be legally shaped.

The employee usually does not have the same upstream support.

There are safeguards. TADM mediation is compulsory before filing. Union members may have union assistance in some cases. The CCT Friend Scheme may allow a family member, friend, or pro bono volunteer to accompany a party with approval. The ECT is judge-led.

But those are not the same as having someone help you understand which legal problem you are really facing.

That is the access asymmetry.

The ECT model matters because ordinary employees need somewhere they can go without hiring a lawyer. But the more complex the law becomes, the more "no lawyers in the room" risks hiding the fact that one side may have had legal help all along.

For solo counsels and small legal teams, that is the practical lesson. The question is not "should everyone lawyer up?" It is: how do you build a responsible first-pass research map when the forum is designed to be accessible, but the law has become dense?

That is where Zeeker becomes interesting.

## What Zeeker Changes

The legal asymmetry is the problem. This is the practical part: how I would use Zeeker to build a first-pass research map before deciding whether deeper paid research, citator checks, or legal advice are needed.

Zeeker is not a paid legal database replacement.

That sentence matters. A Lexis-style search still gives professional comfort: authoritative coverage, citator treatment, and a familiar legal research workflow for checking whether a case remains good law. If the question is final authority checking, use the tool built for that.

Zeeker does something different. It lets you bring public Singapore legal data into an AI tool you already own and understand. In the same workspace, you can search, summarise, translate, compare, and structure the material.

For a solo counsel or small legal team, that changes the first pass.

Start with the known citation. In Zeeker, the fast path is not broad full-text search. It is a structured lookup in the `judgments` table:

```text
database: zeeker-judgements
table: judgments
filter: citation exact "[2026] SGECT 1"
```

That returns the case: JGP v JGQ, ECT/11019/2024, decided on 15 May 2026, with 414 fragments. There was no generated summary when I checked. That is not a failure. It tells you the next move: use fragments.

With the `source_url`, search within `judgments_fragments`. For example, searching within the same document for "act in-person" pulls the key paragraph where the tribunal explains the institutional constraint: parties act in person, and the tribunal does not get opposing counsel's arguments on legal issues.

That is already better than a blind keyword search.

The workflow becomes:

1. Retrieve the exact case by citation.
2. Pull the key fragments inside the same judgment.
3. Identify the legal labels: wrongful dismissal, disciplinary procedure, discretionary bonus, just cause or excuse.
4. Search adjacent cases and materials using those labels.
5. Summarise the results into a research map.
6. Escalate anything that needs paid database treatment, citator checks, or legal advice.

The comparison with a plain Lexis-style search is not "Zeeker wins". It is more specific than that:

| Research need | Paid legal search | Zeeker MCP workflow |
|---|---|---|
| Check authority and treatment | Strong | Not the main job |
| Retrieve a known public case | Strong | Strong if indexed |
| Search within one judgment | Strong | Strong through fragments |
| Summarise or translate | Usually separate workflow | Native to the AI workspace |
| Build a first-pass issue map | Mostly manual | Can happen in the same workspace |
| Work within budget constraints | Subscription-dependent | Lower-friction, subject to coverage |

This is the value: not legal advice, not final authority, not magic.

A map.

For this case, the map starts with a simple observation. The judgment is not mainly about whether the employees did something wrong. It is about how the employer framed that wrong, what had to be proven, and how statutory wrongful dismissal now sits beside the common law version of the same phrase.

That is exactly the kind of distinction a resource-constrained team needs to surface early.

Zeeker does not tell you whether you will win. It helps you stop asking the wrong first question.

## Search, Structure, Compare, Escalate

The safer framework is not "how to assess your dismissal claim". That sounds too much like advice, and it is not what this post is trying to do.

The framework is a research workflow:

**Search.** Start with the citation if you have it. If not, search by the judgment's own vocabulary. Here, that vocabulary includes "wrongful dismissal", "disciplinary procedures", "discretionary bonuses", and "just cause or excuse".

**Structure.** Sort what you find into buckets: statutes, guidelines, cases, propositions, factual patterns, and open questions. Do not collapse everything into "fair" or "unfair".

**Compare.** Compare legal propositions before comparing facts. A case about whether guidelines create a civil cause of action does different work from a case about whether dishonesty was proven.

**Escalate.** Mark what the first pass cannot answer. Is the case still good law? Is there a paid citator signal? Is the claim outside the ECT's jurisdiction? Is there a factual issue that needs advice rather than research?

This is modest. That is why it is useful.

The ECT's no-lawyer model reduces one barrier to entry. Tools like Zeeker can reduce another: the blank page before you know what you are researching.

Neither replaces judgment.

But for solo counsels and small teams, a better first pass can be the difference between spending three hours in the wrong search results and spending 20 minutes finding the right question.

That is enough to matter.
