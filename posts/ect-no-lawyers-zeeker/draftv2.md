I read the CNA headline first. Six employees won compensation. Medical-benefit claims. Vitamins, supplements, skincare.

My first instinct was not certainty. It was discomfort.

If your first reaction was "how did they win?", I don't think that is unreasonable. It was close to mine. One employee had submitted 62 reimbursement claims worth nearly $10,000. About 40 employees were dismissed after an internal review. The facts look bad before any legal vocabulary touches them.

But that reaction is not enough to understand the case.

From a legal standpoint, the hard question was not whether the claims looked bad. It was whether the employer proved the reason it had chosen to rely on: serious breaches committed intentionally, wilfully, or dishonestly. That is a different question. A more technical question. A question that depends on legal labels, evidential burdens, statutory wording, and remedies.

That is what made *JGP v JGQ* [2026] SGECT 1 interesting to me.

The same problem cuts the other way. If you are an employee hoping to prove wrongful dismissal, a sympathetic factual story is not enough. "I was treated unfairly" may be true. It may even be compelling. But the forum still has to ask what kind of wrongful dismissal claim this is, what has to be proven, what evidence matters, and what remedy is available.

Once you see that, you need research.

Not a 20-page memo. Not a paid database dive at the first step. But some way to move from "the facts feel strong" to "these are the legal questions I actually need to understand".

The tribunal itself names the difficulty. The judgment opens by acknowledging that these cases had to be decided in a setting where parties act in person, so the tribunal does not get opposing counsel's arguments on legal questions. Yet the written grounds run to 414 fragments, with a full doctrinal annex on the relationship between common law and statutory wrongful dismissal.

That is the part I could not shake.

The Employment Claims Tribunals are meant to be accessible. Lawyers are generally not allowed. Parties present their own cases. Proceedings are private. The forum is supposed to be speedy, low-cost, and simpler than ordinary litigation.

But "no lawyers in the room" does not mean "no legal advantage".

## What Happened

The facts are uncomfortable, which is why the public reaction is understandable.

In late 2023, the employer started an internal review of medical-benefit claims linked to a clinic anonymised as Clinic X. Employees had been claiming items like vitamins, supplements, and skincare products. The review led to a disciplinary process. Around 40 employees were eventually dismissed.

Eight filed claims in the ECT. One withdrew. Another proceeded before a different tribunal. Six related cases came before Tribunal Magistrate Jared Kang Chern Wey, who issued detailed written grounds using one claimant's case, Ms C's, as the main vehicle.

The numbers are not small:

- 62 reimbursement claims by Ms C over seven months
- almost $10,000 claimed for items including vitamins, supplements, and skincare
- about 40 employees dismissed after the wider review
- S$17,332 awarded to Ms C, plus costs and disbursements

The employer's case was not merely that the claims were wrong. It had framed the dismissals around serious breaches committed intentionally, wilfully, or dishonestly. The tribunal found that the employer had not proven dishonesty, recklessness, or wilful blindness. Ms C had committed a lesser wrong — negligent reliance on practice rather than formal policy — but that did not amount to just cause or excuse for dismissal.

That distinction matters.

The point is not whether the employees were blameless. The point is that the difference between "wrong", "dismissible", and "proven dishonest" is legal work.

## The Public Read and the Lawyer Read

The public read is easy.

You see "skincare", "$10,000", "medical benefits", and "compensation". The instinct is: how did the employees win? That was the shape of some business-facing commentary. Chris J Reed called the result a travesty and focused on the intuitive unfairness of paying out on claims of that kind.

The mainstream news version was narrower. CNA explained that the tribunal found the employer had not proven dishonesty. The Independent Singapore turned the lesson toward workplace practices and unclear policies.

Employment lawyers read the case differently.

Nicholas Ngo focused on the doctrinal problem. "Wrongful dismissal" now lives in two places. In court, the common law claim is anchored in breach. In the ECT, the statutory test asks whether the dismissal was without just cause or excuse. That creates questions about no-reasons dismissals, evidential burden, and what an employer's silence now costs.

Clarence Ding took a different view. He thought the tribunal got it wrong and was glad the employer had filed applications to appeal. But even his disagreement helped the point. He still accepted that statutory wrongful dismissal claims have become complex and unclear, and called for more published judgments.

So the case has at least three readings:

- the moral frame: they claimed skincare
- the HR frame: policy controls and workplace practice failed
- the legal frame: what reason was given, what had to be proven, and what kind of wrongful dismissal claim is this?

This is where the ECT design starts to feel strained.

Not because the tribunal should suddenly become ordinary litigation. That would defeat the point. But because the same facts produce radically different readings depending on whether the reader understands the legal architecture underneath.

If understanding that architecture is necessary, research becomes part of access.

## The Design Problem

When the ECT launched in 2017, the State Courts described it as a speedy, low-cost forum with simplified, judge-led procedures. No lawyers would be involved. The reason was practical: because procedures were simplified, the ECT would not be a forum for complicated employment claims.

That design is important.

If every employment claim required lawyers, many employees would never bring claims at all. Salary disputes and wrongful dismissal claims often sit in the painful middle: too important to ignore, too small to justify full litigation, too urgent to wait years.

Keeping the ECT lawyer-light is not wrong.

But [2026] SGECT 1 shows the irony. The tribunal published a 414-fragment judgment with a doctrinal annex on the law of dismissals in Singapore. It explained that the label "wrongful dismissal" now covers both a common law action and a statutory claim under s 14(2) of the Employment Act. It said the boundaries between substance, process, forum, and remedy are not consistently understood.

This is not the kind of complexity most people associate with a simplified tribunal.

The access gap is not only about who appears at the hearing. It starts earlier.

A large employer may have HR teams, compliance officers, internal escalation processes, in-house counsel, or outside employment lawyers shaping the file long before anyone steps into the tribunal. Even if no lawyer ever appears, the investigation, disciplinary framing, correspondence, appeal handling, and evidence bundle may already be legally shaped.

The employee usually does not have the same upstream support.

There are safeguards. TADM mediation is compulsory before filing. Union members may have union assistance in some cases. The CCT Friend Scheme may allow a family member, friend, or pro bono volunteer to accompany a party with approval. The ECT is judge-led.

But none of those are the same as having someone help you understand which legal problem you are really facing.

That is the access asymmetry.

The ECT model matters because ordinary employees need somewhere they can go without hiring a lawyer. But the more complex the law becomes, the more "no lawyers in the room" risks hiding the fact that one side may have had legal help all along.

For solo counsels and small legal teams, that is the practical lesson. The question is not "should everyone lawyer up?" It is: how do you build a responsible first-pass research map when the forum is designed to be accessible, but the law has become dense?

That is where Zeeker becomes interesting — an open Model Context Protocol (MCP) server that indexes Singapore public judgments and their fragments, and makes them queryable from any AI tool you already use, like Claude or ChatGPT.

## What I Actually Did

This is the part I want to show, not describe.

I started with the citation, not a broad search. Inside an MCP-capable AI tool, the request to Zeeker is structured roughly like this:

```
zeeker.judgments where citation = "[2026] SGECT 1"
```

That syntax is the request shape, not the literal MCP tool call — Zeeker's actual tools are typed function invocations the client makes on your behalf. What came back was a single row:

- citation: `[2026] SGECT 1`
- case name: `JGP v JGQ`
- case number: `ECT/11019/2024`
- decision date: 15 May 2026
- court: SGECT
- subject tags: disciplinary procedures; discretionary bonuses; "wrongful dismissal"
- source URL: elitigation.sg/gd/s/2026_SGECT_1
- fragments: 414
- summary: not yet generated

The missing summary mattered. It changed the next move.

A generated summary, when present, is the easy entry point. With none available, the fast path was fragment search — querying the `judgments_fragments` table for content within the same `source_url`. Searching that table for "act in-person" inside this judgment returned paragraph [4]: the tribunal's own statement that it was working within a setting where parties act in person, so it did not benefit from opposing counsel's arguments on legal issues, and that it considered a doctrinal exposition crucial anyway given the volume of wrongful dismissal claims and the risk of inconsistent outcomes.

That paragraph is the access-to-justice argument in the tribunal's own voice. I would not have found it as cleanly through a broad full-text search.

A second fragment query against the doctrinal annex pulled A.48, where the tribunal sets out the two-meanings problem: "wrongful dismissal" now covers both the common law action and the statutory claim under s 14(2) of the Employment Act, and the boundaries between substance, process, forum, and remedy are not consistently understood.

Two anchor passages from one judgment, in under ten minutes of querying, with paragraph numbers and structure preserved. Easy to drop into a research note. Easy to feed back into the same AI workspace to summarise, translate, or compare against other cases.

The workflow was:

1. Retrieve the exact case by citation.
2. Pull anchor fragments inside the same judgment.
3. Identify the legal labels those fragments surface — wrongful dismissal, disciplinary procedure, discretionary bonus, just cause or excuse, s 14(2).
4. Search adjacent cases using those labels.
5. Summarise the results, in the same AI workspace, into a first-pass research map.
6. Mark what the first pass cannot responsibly answer.

The comparison with a paid Singapore legal database — for me, that means LawNet — is more specific than "Zeeker wins":

The honest summary: paid legal databases remain the place I go for citator treatment and final authority checking. That is not a job Zeeker is built for. What Zeeker changes is the first hour — the gap between "a 414-fragment judgment dropped" and "I know which legal questions actually matter".

For this case, the map starts with one observation. The judgment is not mainly about whether the employees did something wrong. It is about how the employer framed that wrong, what had to be proven, and how statutory wrongful dismissal now sits beside the common law version of the same phrase.

That is exactly the kind of distinction a resource-constrained team needs to surface early.

Zeeker does not tell you whether you will win. It helps you stop asking the wrong first question.

## Search, Structure, Compare, Escalate

The framework is not "how to assess your dismissal claim". That sounds like advice. It is a research workflow, anchored to this case so you can see the moves rather than read the verbs.

**Search.** Start with the citation if you have it. For this case, querying the `citation` column for "[2026] SGECT 1" returned the row in one call. Without a citation, the next-best path is the judgment's own vocabulary: "wrongful dismissal", "disciplinary procedures", "discretionary bonuses", "just cause or excuse". Avoid the broad employment-law full-text search; it returns too much for a first pass.

**Structure.** Sort what comes back into buckets. For this case the buckets were: the Employment Act (especially s 14(2) wrongful dismissal); the Tripartite Guidelines on Wrongful Dismissal; reported decisions on common law wrongful dismissal; tribunal-level reasoning on burden of proof; and open propositions — like whether the Tripartite Guidelines create civil causes of action — that the judgment raises without finally resolving.

**Compare.** Compare legal propositions before comparing facts. A case about whether guidelines create a civil cause of action does different work from a case about whether dishonesty was proven on the evidence. The medical-claims facts in *JGP v JGQ* are not the load-bearing part of the precedent. The doctrinal annex on the two meanings of wrongful dismissal is.

**Escalate.** Mark what the first pass cannot answer. For this case: are the leave-to-appeal applications going to succeed, and what would that change? Has any superior court since revisited the s 14(2) test? Does the discretionary-bonus reasoning extend to non-discretionary commission claims? Those are paid-database questions, citator questions, or advice questions. Not first-pass questions.

This is modest. That is why it is useful.

The ECT's no-lawyer model reduces one barrier to entry. Tools like Zeeker can reduce another: the blank page before you know what you are researching.

Neither replaces judgment.

If the law is now complex enough that employment lawyers are openly debating the first published ECT decision, the real access question is not whether a party can enter the tribunal alone. It is whether anyone in the room — including the solo counsel quietly advising from the side — can find the right map before the hearing.
