# Outline v2: When "No Lawyers" Meets a 414-Fragment Judgment

## Centre of Gravity

Someone who just got a dismissal letter — or suspects one is coming — wants to understand how the ECT actually works. They can't afford a consult. They Google it. They get HR blogs and general explainers. None of that is the tribunal speaking in its own voice.

[2026] SGECT 1 is the tribunal speaking in its own voice. At length. In plain terms. Because the parties didn't have lawyers, the tribunal *wrote out the doctrine* so the judgment would be useful. 414 fragments.

Zeeker makes that judgment queryable. That is the value proposition.

> The forum that said you don't need a lawyer just produced a 414-fragment judgment explaining exactly how it thinks. You can now ask it questions.

## Working Thesis

The ECT's "no lawyers" design is meant to lower barriers to access. But `[2026] SGECT 1` reveals that the legal questions inside the tribunal are not simple — and that someone arriving without legal context is still operating in the dark, even when the forum is designed for them. Zeeker closes the first gap: it lets anyone query the tribunal's own reasoning, in plain language, before deciding whether legal advice is needed.

## Primary Reader

Someone who just received a dismissal letter, or is watching one come. Not necessarily legally trained. Wants to understand: do I have a claim? What does the tribunal actually care about? What did the employer need to prove?

Secondary readers: solo counsels and small legal teams who want to see the practical workflow; union officers helping members.

## Structure

### 1. Opening: The letter

Open not with LinkedIn, but with the person who will never see the LinkedIn debate.

The ECT handled thousands of claims last year. Most of those claimants didn't see the commentary. They got a letter, went to TADM, and either settled or filed. Many had no idea what their employer needed to prove — or whether the reason they were dismissed even matched the legal test the tribunal would apply.

`[2026] SGECT 1` is the first written ECT judgment since 2017. It exists because six of those people pushed all the way to a decision. The tribunal wrote out its reasoning — at length — because it felt the doctrine needed clarification.

*Function: establish that the post speaks to anyone who might end up in the ECT, not just lawyers reading LinkedIn.*

### 2. What the public saw — and what it missed

The case involved about 40 employees dismissed after a medical-benefit claims review. Eight filed ECT claims. Six won compensation before this tribunal.

Three frames competed in public:

- **Moral frame** (business and general public): They claimed skincare and vitamins. How did they win?
- **HR frame** (The Independent): The employer's investigation and policy controls failed.
- **Legal frame** (employment lawyers): What reason did the employer choose to rely on? Who bore the burden of proving it? Was dishonesty actually proven?

Only the third frame is the one the tribunal decided. The difference between "wrong" and "dismissible for dishonesty" is legal work most people can't see without access to the judgment.

*Function: show that public framing systematically hides the legal question.*

### 3. What the ECT was designed to be, and what this judgment is

The 2017 launch: ECT would be low-cost, speedy, accessible. Lawyers not permitted as of right. The tribunal would guide parties through.

[2026] SGECT 1: the tribunal says in paragraph [4] that it had to decide these cases without opposing counsel's arguments on points of law. It wrote this judgment — 414 fragments — because the law needed doctrinal clarity, and the volume of wrongful dismissal claims meant inconsistent outcomes were a real risk.

The irony is named by the tribunal itself: a forum built for simplicity produced a judgment that requires legal context to read.

Neither is wrong. The ECT's accessible design may be the only reason some employees can bring claims at all. But "no lawyers in the room" never meant "no legal complexity in the file."

*The irony the post holds:*
> The ECT was designed so you wouldn't need a lawyer. The first judgment it ever published exists because the tribunal needed to explain, in detail, a body of law that lawyers publicly disagree about.

### 4. The information gap — what you need to know vs. what you can find

Someone arriving at the ECT needs to understand, at minimum:

- What reason did the employer give for dismissal?
- What did the employer need to prove about that reason?
- Which legal framework applies — statutory or common law wrongful dismissal?
- What remedy is actually available at the ECT?

What they can find by Googling: general explainers, HR blog posts, MOM guidance, a CNA article about skincare claims.

What they cannot find without access to the judgment: the tribunal's own explanation of burden of proof, the distinction between the two wrongful dismissal frameworks, the doctrinal analysis of "just cause or excuse."

The gap is not a failure of search effort. The judgment is publicly available on eLitigation — as a PDF, 414 fragments long, written for a legal reader. Accessing it and understanding what it says are two different things.

*Function: establish the precise gap that Zeeker closes.*

**"What is wrongful dismissal in Singapore?"**

**"What happens if the employer doesn't give reasons?"**

The person asking these questions is not running a research session. They are trying to understand what they walked into.

*Key line:*
> Zeeker does not give legal advice. It gives you the tribunal's own words, in response to your own questions, before you decide whether you need a lawyer to interpret them.

### 6. What Zeeker doesn't do — and where you still need a lawyer

Plain limits:

- Zeeker does not tell you whether you will win.
- It does not assess the strength of your specific facts.
- It does not substitute for advice about what to do next.
- Its coverage is still growing; not every case is indexed.

Where you still need a lawyer:
- Deciding whether to settle at TADM
- Understanding whether your employer's stated reason matches the legal test they needed to meet
- Appeals and leave-to-appeal applications
- Any situation where the stakes warrant it

*Key line:*
> Zeeker replaces the blank page before you know whether you need a lawyer. It does not replace the lawyer.

### 7. Closing: Access isn't just entry to the room

The ECT gave employees somewhere to go without hiring a lawyer. That matters.

But access to the tribunal is not the same as access to its map. The first written ECT judgment exists because the tribunal decided the doctrine was unclear enough that it needed to say so in public, at length, without the benefit of adversarial legal argument to help it.

That judgment is now queryable. The same legal reasoning the tribunal wrote out because the parties in the room couldn't get it from counsel — you can now ask it questions.

That is not a revolution. It is the forum's own work, made accessible.
