The meter showed up on a switch I'd been flipping for free.

Cowork lives as a toggle on a tab in the Microsoft 365 chat app. I'd been turning it on for weeks, handing it work, quietly building a workflow around the way it ran. I never saw a number. Then on 16 June, Microsoft pushed Cowork to general availability, and the toggle started counting. Every model call, every document it reaches for, every tool it picks up, every minute it runs — metered in Copilot Credits at a cent each, on top of the $30 seat I was already paying.

I got rugpulled.

I'm rebuilding the workflow now, around a cost I didn't have to think about a month ago. And here's the part that stings: I can't just walk away from it. My colleagues like Cowork. They reach for it without being told to. Whatever I think about the pricing, the tool has done the one thing most legal AI never manages — people actually use it.

So I spent two weeks irritated. Then, somewhere in the rebuild, irritation turned into something closer to relief. Because the meter isn't the disaster everyone's calling it. For people like me, it might be the best thing to happen in a while.

## The buffet is closing everywhere

Cowork isn't a one-off. The same week felt like a turning of the season:

- Microsoft moved Cowork from a free-feeling preview to metered GA.
- Legora moved its most capable product, Agent Pro, off per-seat pricing — roughly $3,000 a seat a year — to consumption.
- The pattern runs past legal entirely: even SAP is shifting enterprise software to consumption as agents eat the old seat model.

Shawn Curran, the CEO of Jylo, put it bluntly [to Artificial Lawyer](https://www.artificiallawyer.com/2026/06/03/legal-ai-has-a-growing-token-price-problem/): "per seat pricing is gone, if Anthropic, Microsoft and OpenAI have moved away from it, no-one is going to subsidise legal tech vendors on all you can eat."

That's the line everyone's repeating. The free buffet is over.

## The consensus, and why it isn't wrong

I want to be fair to the gloom, because it isn't stupid.

*The Lawyer* ran a piece this month under the headline "Law's AI honeymoon is over." Developers got a preview of the mood two weeks before I did, when GitHub Copilot went usage-based and the bills landed — one developer watched a single change cost six dollars and called the spend "impossible to predict," and some monthly costs jumped from $29 to nearly $750. The fear is real, and it has receipts.

The reason underneath it is real too. Agents don't sit quietly. They plan, call models, check their own work, and keep going until the task is done, and the compute they burn varies wildly from one matter to the next. Microsoft's own people admitted Cowork couldn't be offered on an unlimited-use basis. When the thing you're selling can run up an unpredictable bill on its own, the flat seat was never going to survive.

So yes — costs are rising, and the vendors aren't villains for passing them on.

## But the timing is the tell

Here's what nags at me. The meter didn't arrive after we'd figured out what legal AI is actually for. It arrived in the middle of the experiment.

We are nowhere near done proving the legal use cases. Singapore's guidance for using generative AI in legal work only landed in March. Half the "legal-specific" features on the market are, as one founder told Artificial Lawyer, "innovation theatre and fear designed to justify pricing." Plenty of us are still finding out which tasks AI genuinely helps with and which just look impressive in a demo.

Free pricing was quietly doing the most important work in the industry: it let people experiment cheaply enough to discover what's worth doing. Metering taxes exactly that. It demands you justify the spend while making the experimentation that would justify it more expensive. You need the answer to afford the question.

Premature, then? It depends who you ask. From a buyer's seat, watching a market still learning what it's for, yes — it's early. From the vendor's books, it's overdue: they're bleeding money on subsidised frontier tokens and can't carry it forever. Neither read is wrong. The honest question isn't "is it premature?" It's "premature for whom?"

But whichever side you sit on, the same thing just happened underneath the argument. The job of proving which legal use cases are worth it didn't disappear. It moved. It used to be the vendor's problem, absorbed into a subscription. Now it's yours, and it has a price tag.

## The meter prices compute, not trust

There's a sharp observation buried in all this, and it comes from someone sceptical of the whole shift. Writing about Legora's move, Elgar Weijtmans made a point I haven't been able to put down: consumption is not outcome.

[Legal AI Won't Be Priced Per Seat. Legora Just Agreed.](https://unfilteredbits.substack.com/p/legal-ai-wont-be-priced-per-seat)

You pay for the work the agent does, not for that work being right. The meter charges you the moment the compute is spent — it has no opinion on whether the output was any good, whether the use case was sound, whether you should have run it at all. As Weijtmans notes, the spending limits these tools ship with give the game away: a threshold is something you set against a cost you can't foresee, not a result you've learned to trust.

That gap — between what you paid and whether it was worth paying — is the whole story. The vendor will not close it for you. They are, in the end, selling you metered compute. Someone still has to stand in that gap and say: this was worth it, and you can trust what came out.

I've written before that AI made building cheap but never made knowing what to build cheap. The meter is that idea with a price tag stapled to it.

[When Building Gets Cheap But Knowing Stays Expensive](https://www.alt-counsel.com/when-building-gets-cheap-but-knowing-stays-expensive/)

## That someone is the lawyer-builder

Think about who can actually make that call.

An engineer can cost the workflow. They can tell you a run will burn 700 credits and how to shave it to 400. What they can't tell you is whether the redline the agent produced is one you'd put your name on.

A lawyer can judge the work. They know whether the output is right, whether it's safe to send. What they often can't do is look under the hood, understand why it cost what it cost, or rebuild it to cost less.

The lawyer-builder does both. And — this is the part that matters in a room full of colleagues — the lawyer-builder is the one people believe when they say "this is trustworthy, and it's the right direction." Not the vendor's sales deck. Not the engineer who can't speak to the legal risk. You, because you've done the legal work and you've built the thing.

I argued a while back that AI won't replace you — someone who decides will. The meter is where deciding stops being a virtue and becomes a line item.

[AI Won't Replace You. Someone Who Decides Will.](https://www.alt-counsel.com/ai-wont-replace-you-someone-who-decides-will/)

When AI felt free, none of this was visible. You could spray it across every task and the bad bets hid inside the flat fee. The meter ends the hiding. And that turns out to be a gift.

Because the meter is an honesty machine.

It makes your good judgment legible. "This contract-risk workflow runs about three dollars a matter and saves two hours" — that used to be a feeling you had to defend in the abstract. Now it's a line item you can point to. The thing that always made you valuable was invisible. The meter renders it.

It makes everyone else's bad judgment visible too. The feature-spray, the demo-ware, the use case that was always theatre — they sat comfortably inside a bundled subscription. Now they come with a receipt.

The catch is the whole point: a meter only tells the truth to someone who can read it. A number on a dashboard is raw data, not judgment. Looking at "$7 a run" and knowing whether the legal output was worth $7, whether a cheaper model would have done the job, whether the use case is substance or theatre — that reading is exactly what the lawyer-builder does. The meter makes judgment legible, and it makes the absence of judgment expensive.

So you stop vouching with vibes. You start vouching with receipts.

## This isn't free money

I don't want to oversell it. The meter is not a gift basket for lawyer-builders. It's a harder game that happens to reward a skill we have.

Consumption pricing can just as easily dump the whole ROI problem on you. Most legal AI vendors are, as another founder put it, "middle men — they buy tokens and resell them at a premium," and the bill they hand you is denominated in credits, not outcomes. In one 2026 survey, only about half of organisations said they could confidently evaluate AI ROI at all. The meter rewards the people who can connect spend to value and punishes the ones who can't. That's not a softer world. It's a more demanding one.

And watch the language. Vendors will sell consumption as if it were outcome — "pay for results." It isn't. You pay for the work, not for it being right. The lawyer-builder is who keeps that honest inside the building.

## For the rest of us

Here's the part that should land for solo counsels and small teams: the buffet was never bottomless for you anyway.

You were always justifying every dollar. You never had the luxury of spraying expensive AI across every task to see what stuck. You already lived in the world where the question was "is this worth it?" before you spent. Metering doesn't trap you. It drags everyone else into the world you were already in — the one where judgment about what's worth doing was always the scarce thing.

I'm still rebuilding my Cowork workflow. I'm still paying for a tool I'd rather have kept free. But I've stopped reading the meter as a punishment.

The meter isn't the end of the party. It's the moment the room finally needs someone who can read the bill.
