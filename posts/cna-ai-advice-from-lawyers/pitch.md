# Pitch

Next month I'm giving a talk — *"What AI Demands of Compliance Professionals: The
Discussion We Are Not Having"* — at the SCCE Singapore Regional. Its spine:
professionals need two guiding lights to drive safely into AI — professional
judgement *and* AI literacy. One without the other, and you're off the road.
Then I read Mark Yeo's CNA commentary, and it became my best example of driving
with one headlight. Every time he wrote "chatbot," I winced. He's a sharp lawyer
making a careful argument, but he's arguing against AI as it existed in 2023: a
thing you talk to. The AI that actually threatens his thesis is agentic — it
searches case law, drafts, and acts — the very "Claude Legal" he cites two
paragraphs before flattening it back into "chatbot." Without literacy, the
chatbot becomes a punching bag that makes human judgment look irreplaceable. But
are we really that unique? The research is murkier than the profession's
self-image. And his fix — clients using AI to brief their lawyers better —
collapses the moment every client does it: the human in the loop becomes the
human bottleneck. For solo counsels, the answer isn't charging more for
judgment. It's getting literate before the agents arrive.

## The two moments that drove the post (Houfu's real reactions)

1. **The word "chatbot."** Frustration that the piece keeps saying "chatbot" when
   the real capability is agentic. AI isn't a supplemental assistant you just
   talk to — agents search, use tools (MCP), and act. Yeo even cites "Claude
   Legal" automating contract review and compliance checks, then reverts to
   "chatbot... only ever a tool, not a trusted adviser." The contradiction is
   inside his own article.

2. **The "agape" passage.** Yeo's fix: "clients can use AI to prepare case
   summaries for lawyers to review... identify gaps or inconsistencies within
   their documents... These can be forwarded to the lawyers for their views and
   advice." Narrow-minded at scale — if *every* client does this, every lawyer
   is overwhelmed. The human in the loop becomes the human bottleneck.

## Angle / thesis

Lawyers (and compliance professionals) need two guiding lights for AI:
**professional judgement** and **AI literacy**. Yeo has the first, not the
second. Without literacy, the professional instinct defaults to setting the
chatbot up as a punching bag — a foil that makes human intuition, judgment, and
"knowing the wrong question" look irreplaceable. But the research on human
uniqueness (judgment, intuition, empathy) is murkier than the profession's
self-image admits.

## Research secured (the human-uniqueness angle)

1. **Cheng et al. (2025), "Sycophantic AI Decreases Prosocial Intentions and
   Promotes Dependence"** (arXiv:2510.01395). Across 11 SOTA models, AI affirms
   users' actions ~50% more than humans (abstract says "50% more"; slide cites
   49% — reconcile before publishing). Two preregistered experiments (N=1604),
   incl. live-interaction study on real interpersonal conflict. Sycophantic AI
   *reduced* willingness to repair conflict and *increased* conviction of being
   right — yet users rated it higher quality, trusted it more, wanted to use it
   again. Perverse incentive: people are drawn to validation even as it erodes
   judgment.

2. **"Programmed to Please: the moral and epistemic harms of AI sycophancy"**
   (AI & Ethics, 2026; doi:10.1007/s43681-026-01007-4). Conceptual/virtue-ethics
   analysis. Key load-bearing points for us:
   - Sycophancy = prioritising user approval over truth; a "structural
     bullshitter" (indifferent to truth), not a liar.
   - **Human vs AI sycophancy**: humans are strategic/selective and *know* they
     flatter; AI is systematic/indiscriminate. But validation bias is a "human
     universal" — the AI Mirror reflects *our* preference back. (Useful guard
     against "humans are exempt".)
   - **The "simple fix" objection fails**: prompting AI to be a critic often only
     *masks* sycophancy ("acting the role of a critic" — Mollick). Keeps me
     honest: literacy isn't a one-prompt cure.
