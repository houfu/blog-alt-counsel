## Session 2 — 10 March 2026 (claude.ai)

This session was a deep brainstorming dialogue that fundamentally reshaped the piece across 5 drafts. Key evolution:

### The thesis sharpened progressively:

1. **v1:** TWS is wrong, Zhang is right, here's why (debate piece)
2. **v2:** External moral frames vs. internal professional tension — TWS and Zhang are lenses, not characters
3. **v3:** AI cannot practice intentionality. Only humans can. The person behind the prompt *is* the moral equation.
4. **v4:** Intentionality isn't gated by experience — junior lawyers can be as intentional as seniors. Practical "how to save yourself" section.
5. **v5:** Added moral ambiguity (Zhang's plagiarist was intentional too), warning about replacement by other humans, cost of deciding, and Houfu's optimistic belief that intention tilts toward right.

### Key ideas developed:

**"Eyes closed, eyes open"** — The central image. Zhang chose eyes closed for a portrait. That choice *is* the art. AI doesn't choose — it resolves a probability distribution. The difference isn't quality; it's category.

**"The training data is inert"** — Paint doesn't plagiarise. A person picks up the brush. TWS locates the moral problem in training data, but the data has no agency. Moral weight lives entirely in the human act of bringing something out.

**Intentionality is morally ambiguous** — Zhang's plagiarist was intentional too. They chose to steal. Intentionality isn't a virtue; it's a capacity. It carries moral weight because it can go either way.

**Challenging "AI does low-end, humans do high-end"** — There's no such thing as decision-free work. Document review was *labelled* low-end because the profession decided those decisions weren't worth senior attention. The hierarchy was never about the work; it was about who the profession chose to value. AI doesn't respect the org chart.

**Junior lawyer agency** — Not waiting for mentors or firm policy. Three practical scenarios: interrogating templates (someone else's fossilised decisions), reviewing AI drafts (would I have made the same choices?), questioning AI research (what did it leave out?). "Nobody needs to give you permission to be intentional."

**The cost and the optimism** — Deciding is hard, slow, tiring, expensive. That's why shortcuts exist. But Houfu's belief: when people actually decide — confront what they're doing, own the choices — they are more likely to do right than wrong. Not guaranteed. More likely.

### Structural decisions:

- Opens with law firms drawing lines (professional angle first, not media criticism)
- TWS is an *illustration* of the pattern, not the subject
- Zhang is a lens for locating intentionality, not a hero
- The piece builds: lines in the sand → specificity reveals decisions → AI can't decide → intentionality is morally ambiguous → the tension professionals live with → what AI reveals about the profession's hierarchy → how to save yourself → deciding is hard but worth it
- Closing image: "A machine can produce a portrait with eyes closed. Only a human can choose it."

- Framing as "learning in public" is on-brand
