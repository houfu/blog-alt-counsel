Last September, I published a blog post critiquing MinLaw's draft Guide for Using Generative AI in the Legal Sector. I shared it on LinkedIn. People I respect — practitioners, legal tech folks — encouraged me to submit it formally. So on September 29, 2025, the night before the consultation closed, I turned my critique into a four-page submission and emailed it to MinLaw.

I felt a bit foolish doing it. The consultation would attract responses from Allen & Gledhill, WongPartnership, Drew & Napier, Google, Microsoft. Some guy nobody has ever heard of sending in his blog post repackaged as formal feedback? I figured it would be politely received and quietly filed.

Six months later, on March 6, 2026, the final guide dropped. I skimmed it. Nothing jumped out immediately — it was longer, more detailed, but I couldn't tell what had actually changed. So I asked Claude to do a side-by-side comparison of my submission against the final text, half-expecting hallucinated matches.

It wasn't hallucinating.

## What the Consultation Produced

The numbers tell part of the story. The draft was 26 pages. The final guide is 48 pages, plus annexes with sample policies, vendor checklists, and engagement letter templates. Senior Minister of State Murali Pillai told Parliament that MinLaw received "over 20 constructive responses."

MinLaw's press release was more specific about what those responses changed. The guide was "enhanced to better support adoption across diverse operating contexts, including smaller law practices and in-house legal teams."

That last phrase caught my attention. My original blog post was titled "The Solo Counsel Reality: What MinLaw's AI Guidelines Miss About In-House Practice." The whole thrust of my submission was that the draft guide didn't work for people like me.

The contributors list in the final guide names roughly 40 organizations — big firms, legaltech providers, government agencies, universities — and exactly three individuals. I'm one of them.

I wasn't the only person who submitted publicly. BetterWiser's Lynette Ooi published a summary of her seven recommendations on Substack. ALITA announced their submission. But most respondents didn't share what they wrote, so it's hard to trace specific changes to specific submissions.

What I can do is trace what I recommended against what changed.

## AI Literacy: From Missing to Mandatory

My biggest criticism of the draft guide was about AI literacy — or rather, its absence.

The draft's paragraph 17 listed three things lawyers should do when using AI: (a) keep a lawyer in the loop, (b) apply greater scrutiny outside their expertise, and (c) remember they bear ultimate responsibility. That was it. No mention of understanding how AI tools actually work. No mention of learning when they fail, or why, or how to prompt them effectively.

The assumption seemed to be that professional judgment alone was sufficient. I argued it wasn't. You can't exercise meaningful oversight over a tool you don't understand. A lawyer who doesn't know that AI confidently fabricates citations isn't exercising professional judgment — they're exercising professional trust.

My submission recommended adding a new sub-paragraph requiring lawyers to "develop AI literacy," with five specific competencies:

1. How different AI tools function and their failure modes
2. When AI tools are likely to produce reliable versus unreliable results
3. Basic prompt techniques that reduce hallucination risks
4. That AI competency varies significantly across different legal tasks
5. When AI output requires verification beyond standard legal review

The final guide's paragraph 20(a) now reads:

> "Develop AI literacy. Legal professionals using GenAI should understand (i) how AI tools function and their limitations, (ii) when AI tools are likely to generate reliable output and when they are not, (iii) basic prompting techniques to reduce hallucination and bias, (iv) that AI competency varies across legal tasks, and (v) when and by whom additional scrutiny should be exercised when reviewing GenAI output."

Five points. Same structure. In several places, near-verbatim language. The draft had nothing like this. The final guide made it a foundational requirement.

I don't know how many other respondents made the same recommendation. Given that over 20 submissions came in, the AI literacy gap was probably obvious to many people. But the close textual alignment suggests my framing contributed to the final language. Either way, the consultation process worked. The draft had a gap, respondents identified it, and the final version filled it.

## Consumer Tools: From Edge Case to Practical Protocol

The second theme in my submission was about consumer AI tools — the ChatGPTs and Claude.ais that practitioners actually use day to day, as opposed to the enterprise platforms that large firms procure.

The draft guide's paragraph 19(c) gave this three lines: review the terms of use, refrain from entering confidential information, anonymise data. That was the entire consumer tool guidance. Three bare sentences for the tools that most lawyers in Singapore were already using.

I argued this created a regulatory gap. The framework assumed organizational deployment — IT committees, procurement processes, security reviews. But a solo counsel using ChatGPT on their phone doesn't have an IT committee. They need specific protocols.

My submission recommended detailed consumer tool guidance: disable data retention, verify privacy settings regularly, use placeholders like [Party A] and [Company B] and [Amount X], frame queries as hypotheticals, use isolated clauses instead of full documents, and understand that temporary retention and re-identification risks persist even with privacy settings enabled.

The final guide's paragraph 23(d) now provides exactly this:

> "When using free-to-use GenAI tools: (i) Disable data retention and use for model training; verify settings regularly as updates may reset configurations; and (ii) Avoid using confidential or commercially sensitive information; if necessary, (i) anonymise data by replacing identifiers and sensitive information with generic placeholders (e.g. [Party A], [Company B], [Amount X]); (ii) frame queries as hypothetical scenarios; and (iii) use isolated clauses instead of full documents where possible."

The same placeholder examples. The same warning about settings resets. The same progression from anonymization to hypothetical framing to isolated clauses. Even the risk notes match: "Data may be temporarily retained even with privacy settings enabled" and "Anonymised content may still be identifiable with sufficient context."

Some of this language is verbatim from my submission. I checked twice.

## Beyond My Submission: What Else Changed

The final guide improved in ways that went well beyond what I recommended. This matters — the document reflects the collective input of over 20 respondents and MinLaw's own development work, not any single submission.

**Bias mitigation** got its own treatment. The draft had one sentence about bias. The final guide identifies three sources of AI bias and adds paragraph 20(e) on mitigation strategies. This wasn't in my submission, and it's an important addition.

**Risk-based oversight** is now visualized in a decision diagram. The final guide distinguishes between human-in-the-loop (lawyer reviews before output is used) and human-on-the-loop (lawyer monitors but doesn't review every output). This is the kind of practical framework that helps practitioners calibrate their oversight to the actual risk level.

**Sample governance policies** fill Annexes C through E — internal AI governance policies, employee handbook clauses, engagement letter templates, and vendor checklists. I find these particularly practical. A solo counsel or small team can adapt these templates rather than drafting governance documents from scratch. That's the difference between guidance that tells you what to do and guidance that helps you do it.

**Real firm examples** now include in-house teams. GenZero's AI implementation is featured alongside the big law examples. The draft had only large firm case studies.

**The scope expanded** to cover legal technologists, alternative legal service providers, and law students. Minister Edwin Tong framed the stakes at the launch event: AI is the "biggest disruptive force to the legal profession."

**Junior lawyer development** got explicit attention. Paragraph 45 addresses the concern that AI could undermine foundational competencies if juniors rely on it too early — a real worry that the draft didn't touch.

## Does It Work for Solo Counsel Now?

My original blog post ended with this line: "Maybe I'm not the target audience for this guide. But that itself is the problem."

So does the final guide fix that problem?

Significantly, yes. The AI literacy requirement and the consumer tool protocols directly address the two gaps I identified. The sample governance policies and templates are genuinely useful for small teams that can't afford to build frameworks from scratch. The expanded scope explicitly includes in-house counsel and smaller practices. The in-house examples make the guide feel less like a document written for firms with 200 lawyers and an innovation lab.

But an implementation support gap remains. The guide tells smaller practices what good AI governance looks like. It even provides templates. But execution still requires time, expertise, and resources that solo counsels and small teams may not have. MinLaw's LIFT (Legal Industry Framework for Transformation) pilot — which provides hands-on implementation support — has only seven firms so far.

The guide is a framework. Frameworks help. But the hardest part for resource-constrained practitioners isn't knowing what to do. It's finding the time and support to actually do it.

## The Consultation Process Works — If You Participate

Here's what I keep coming back to. I almost didn't submit. I assumed my perspective wouldn't matter alongside the institutional responses. I was wrong.

I'm not claiming sole credit for any of these changes. Over 20 respondents submitted feedback, and many probably identified the same gaps I did. MinLaw's team did substantial independent work expanding the guide. The final document reflects a genuine consultative process, not any single voice.

But the textual alignment between my submission and the final language is hard to dismiss entirely. At minimum, my submission was one data point that reinforced a direction MinLaw was already considering. At maximum, some of my specific framing made it into the final text.

Either way, the lesson is the same. Public consultations aren't performative exercises. They're real opportunities to shape the frameworks that govern our profession. Write publicly. Submit formally. You don't need to be a big firm or an institution.

The frameworks that govern us shouldn't be written only by the people with the biggest teams.
